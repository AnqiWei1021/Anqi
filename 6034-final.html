Individual Data Analysis “Exploring How Social Media Shapes My Daily Behaviour” (January 18 – April 10, 2026)

Introduction

In today’s digital era of information explosion, the smartphone has already become an important part of our daily lives. For many people, a smartphone is not a communication tool; it has gradually occupied a lot of people’s time and attention. Nowadays, personal daily behaviour and life rhythm are also increasingly influenced by digital media, especially social media. This high dependence raises an important question: to what extent does social media affect and shape our daily behaviour patterns? Based on this background, this study is based on my personal data, collected and analyzed my daily smartphone usage from January 18 to April 10, 2026. The data content includes daily screen usage time, the number of notifications, pickups, steps, and the usage duration of different applications. Through the visualization and analysis of these data, this study tries to explore the proportion of social media in personal time, and smartphone usage behaviour, whether influencing real-life activities (like walking and sports). Finally, the analysis results show that social applications account for an extremely high proportion of my smartphone usage, especially TikTok, WeChat, and RedNote applications. The interesting thing is that the total usage time of TikTok within three months reached 235.85 hours, which means I spend 3 to 5 hours on average per day in this application. These results not only indicated my personal high commitment to digital video entertainment, but also reflected that social media has a strong appeal and potential influence in my daily life.

Data & Method

This study is based on my personal smartphone usage data from January 18 to April 10, 2026. Data mainly comes from the "Screen Time" feature built into the iPhone, and this function operates on a cycle of 7 days. It statistically analyzes users' screen usage, including the total daily usage time and each application’s proportion of usage time. In addition, the step count data comes from the Health App on the phone and is used to reflect the level of daily physical activity. In terms of data processing, I first organized all the raw data into Excel tables and imported them into Datawrapper for visual analysis. Although initially I tried to use Flourish and Datawrapper together to make visualisations to ensure the consistency and clarity of data presentation, finally I chose Datawrapper to generate all charts. In terms of data integrity, some data in this study were missing from March 10th to March 15th, 2026. To ensure the continuity of the data analysis, I used the average of neighbouring dates to fill in the missing data. At the same time, combined with my personal activities record (such as phone photos, etc.) to guess that time the screens and applications usage time. This processing method, to some extent, improves the data’s integrity, but it also might bring some errors.

Findings

1. Screen Time

It can be seen from the line graph of screen usage time that my daily screen usage time shows a distinct fluctuating trend. As a whole, most of the time was concentrated between 6 and 10 hours, but there were significant peaks on some dates. On 27 Feb, the screen usage time was close to 16 hours, forming the highest point of the entire cycle, which reflected the high dependence on smartphones on certain days. In addition, there was also a relatively obvious downward trend in the middle and late February. This change is closely related to my personal life arrangement, because it was during the Chinese New Year, I went on a trip, and my real activities were more focused on real life, thus reducing my reliance on the smartphone. This shows that the screen usage time is not only influenced by personal habitat, but also related to specific life situations. Overall, this chart shows my smartphone usage time has strong volatility, and it also provides a basis for the subsequent analysis of its relationship with step count, notifications, and usage habits.

2. Step Count

This chart shows the changes in my daily step count over a three-month period. As a whole, the number of steps was roughly distributed between 18 and 17000, with a large fluctuation range, which indicates that my body’s activity level has obviously differed on different dates, and these changes were mainly influenced by the itinerary arrangement of the day. To be specific, the steps count often increases significantly when I plan or arrange an outing, but on days without clear arrangements, I prefer to stay indoors and take fewer steps, which shows my body’s activity more depends on a specific schedule, rather than a stable daily habit. From an overall trend perspective, my steps lack some continuity and regularity, and the level of daily activities shows an uneven characteristic. This pattern also reflects that my exercise behavior is more phased rather than a long-term and stable habit.

3. Scatter

This scatter plot mainly shows the relationship between my daily screen time and step count over three months. From the overall distribution perspective, the majority of the data are concentrated in the screen usage time range of 6 to 10 hours per day, and the highest day even exceeded 16 hours, indicating that in some cases, my smartphone almost occupied most of my daily time. Further observation reveals that when the screen usage time is within the main range of 6 to 10 hours, my daily steps are roughly distributed between 5,000 and 12,000. This phenomenon shows that even with a certain level of physical activity, my smartphone usage time still remains at a relatively high level, suggesting that I still frequently use my smartphone during daily traveling, walking, or sports, such as checking information or browsing social media. However, from the whole perspective, the distribution of scattered points in the graph is rather scattered and does not show a distinct linear relationship, which shows there are not have strongly relativity between screen usage time and steps; a higher number of steps does not necessarily correspond to a lower screen time; in many cases, both occur simultaneously. Therefore, this graph is more inclined to reflect a feature of "parallel behavior", that is, the use of smartphones has been embedded in daily activities rather than just occurring in a static state.

4. App Usage

This chart shows the total usage time of various mobile applications I have spent in the past three months. To conduct a clearer analysis, I have classified all applications into four categories: Entertainment, Social, Utility, and Productivity. From the perspective of overall distribution, entertainment and social applications occupy the vast majority of usage time. Among them, TikTok has the highest proportion among all applications, with a cumulative usage time of 235.85 hours over three months. The second is the social application WeChat, with a usage time of 127.26 hours. This result indicates that short-video platforms and instant messaging software have occupied a core position in my daily life, and also reflects the high attractiveness and stickiness of current digital media content, especially short videos. In addition, applications such as Rednote, WhatsApp, and ChatGPT also show a relatively high usage frequency, indicating that my usage habits are not limited to entertainment but also involve information acquisition and communication. Tool-type applications such as Photo and Google Maps also have a certain usage duration, reflecting the functional role of the smartphone in daily life. It is worth noting that the core applications with a relatively high usage time (such as TikTok, WeChat, and Rednote) are all developed in China. This also indicates from the side that my social networking and content consumption are mainly concentrated on these platforms.

5. Pickups

This chart shows the comparison between my daily screen usage time and the number of times I picked up my smartphone within three months. From the whole perspective, I pick up my phone about 100 times a day, with the lowest being around 43 times and the highest reaching 184 times. This indicates that I have a relatively frequent habit of checking my phone. However, after comparing the number of times it is picked up with the screen usage time, it can be found that there is no obvious correlation between the two. On some days with lower screen usage time, the number of times picked up remained at a relatively high level, while on some days with longer usage time, the number of times picked up did not increase significantly either. This phenomenon indicates that my smartphone usage is not only reflected in the total duration but also in the frequent short-term checking behavior. That is to say, smartphones have become a tool that can be picked up and checked at any time, rather than being used intensively within a specific period of time.

6. Notifications

This graph shows the relationship between the number of notifications and screen usage time. From the overall trend, there is no obvious correlation between the two, and the change is closer to a random distribution. On some dates with more notifications, my screen usage time is actually lower. On the contrary, when there are fewer notifications, screen usage time has increased. This phenomenon shows that notifications do not necessarily directly drive my smartphone usage behavior; in contrast, when there is a lack of notification reminders, I am more likely to proactively check my phone to confirm if I have missed any messages, which leads to an increase in usage time. This behavior reflects a potential habit of "proactive inspection" rather than merely passively responding to notifications. Therefore, compared with the number of notifications themselves, users' psychological expectations and usage habits may have a greater impact on screen usage time.

Discussion

Through a comprehensive analysis of the six charts, we can find that the use of smartphones has occupied a very important position in my daily life. Among them, entertainment and social applications account for a relatively high proportion in overall usage and show a dominant position. From the whole perspective, my smartphone usage is not only reflected in the relatively long duration of use, but it is also manifested as frequent and fragmented usage habits, and this pattern has already been integrated into my daily life, rather than using a single tool.

First, from the usage structure of the application's perspective, social media and entertainment occupied a lot of time. TikTok three month total usage time achieved 235 hours, which is far superior to other applications, and social software such as WeChat also accounts for a relatively high proportion. This phenomenon reflects that short-video platforms and social media occupy a dominant position in digital life. especially on TikTok, algorithmic curation through features like the "Feed for You" effectively captures user attention, leading to deep immersion in the ecosystem (Qin, 2026). This “short, fast, and continuous” content structure enables users to extend their usage time and to some extent explains my frequent use of TikTok.

Secondly, from the perspective of usage behavior patterns, my smartphone usage is not only reflected in long-term continuous use, but also in frequent "fragmented viewing." The average number of times I pick up my smartphone each day is about 100, and the highest can even reach over 180 times. However, there is no obvious correlation between this frequency and screen usage time. Similarly, there is also a lack of direct connection between the number of notifications and their usage time. When there are fewer notifications, I am actually more likely to proactively check my phone to confirm if there is any missing information, which indicates that my smartphone usage behavior has shifted from "passively receiving information" to "actively checking information", forming a habitual and even semi-automated behavioral pattern. According to Milyavskaya et al. (2018), this behavior is a typical manifestation of "fear of missing out" (FOMO), which functions as a self-regulatory failure that drives constant social monitoring. This explains the worry about possibly missing information, which prompts me to constantly check my phone.

Thirdly, from the body activities perspective, I originally expected that there would be a certain negative correlation between the number of steps taken and the time spent on the screen, that is, when I walked more, the time spent on my phone should be reduced. However, the data results do not support this assumption. The scatter plot shows that there is no obvious correlation between the two, that is, on days with a higher step count, screen usage time may still remain at a relatively high level. This phenomenon aligns with Berry’s (2011) discussion on the "computational turn," which suggests that digital technology has increasingly mediated our research and daily lives, becoming an ever-present "background" that structures our experiences. This indicates that the use of smartphones is no longer confined to a static state but has extended to daily travel processes, such as frequent use of smartphones while walking or going out, and digital behavior and real behavior do not replace each other but rather present an overlapping or even coexisting relationship.

Fourthly, from a further perspective of lifestyle, the changes in my step count show considerable volatility; it lacks a stable exercise habit. Whether to engage in outdoor activities or walk depends more on whether there are specific arrangements for the day, rather than forming a fixed daily routine. Without any arrangements, I prefer to stay indoors and use my smartphone, which to some extent reinforces the central position of the smartphone in daily life. It can be said that while smartphones fill in the gaps of time, they have gradually influenced my pace of life and behavioral choices.

However, this study also has certain limitations. Firstly, the data is only derived from individual samples and lacks representativeness, making it impossible to generalize to a broader population; Secondly, the research period was only three months, which is relatively short and may not reflect long-term behavioral patterns. In addition, there were some omissions during the data collection process. For instance, the data from March 10th to 15th was filled in through the average value, which might have a certain impact on the results. Therefore, when interpreting these results, a certain degree of caution needs to be maintained.

To sum up, this study indicated that the smartphone is not a simple tool, it has gradually integrated into my daily life. Especially under the influence of social media and short-video platforms, my usage habits are characterized by high frequency and fragmentation, which to some extent have also influenced my time allocation and lifestyle. These results not only reflect my personal usage habits, but also, from one aspect, demonstrate the influence of the current digital environment on individual behavior.

Conclusion

This study, by analyzing my smartphone usage data over three months, reveals the complex relationship between personal digital behavior and daily life. The results show that social media, especially platforms represented by TikTok and WeChat, dominate my smartphone usage and have largely influenced my way of time allocation. Meanwhile, the use of smartphones shows the characteristics of high frequency and fragmentation, indicating that it has been deeply integrated into daily behaviors rather than merely being a tool. Furthermore, the research found that there is no significant direct relationship between screen usage time and the number of steps or notifications. This indicates that smartphone usage behavior is not driven by a single factor but is jointly influenced by multiple factors such as personal habits, content appeal, and life scenarios. Especially the continuous attraction of short video content makes mobile phone usage more continuous and immersive. Overall, this research not only helped me gain a clearer understanding of my own mobile phone usage patterns but also made me aware of the potential impact of digital behavior on real life. In the future, I may need to be more conscious in managing my mobile phone usage to achieve a healthier balance between digital life and real life.
